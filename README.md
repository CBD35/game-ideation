From Zero to Game Developer — Building with AI
The Story Behind These Projects
I started this with no coding background. A government degree specialising in counter-terrorism, military reserves service, years in theater directing and lighting, and a lifelong love of gaming — but no experience writing code.
During the Passover break of 2025, in the middle of a difficult personal and national period, I decided to use the time differently. Instead of waiting for the right opportunity to appear, I'd build something. I'd always been drawn to games. I'd always wanted to understand how they were made. So I opened a conversation with Claude and said: teach me.
What followed was several weeks of building, breaking, iterating, and directing. Every project on this page grew out of that process.

The Projects
game3 and game4: Clicker into Gold of a Dragon
What it is: An idle clicker. Click to earn gold, buy upgrades that earn gold automatically. The Dragon Hoard upgrade — which I added myself — gives +20 gold per second and costs 5,000 gold.
How it started: This was the very first piece of code I touched. I was shown a basic clicker and told to make one change on my own. I added the Dragon Hoard upgrade and changed some colours. Small as that sounds, it was the first time I made code do something I decided, not something I was told to copy.
What I took from it: A rough sense of how variables and functions work — that there's data (the numbers), and there are actions (the things that change the numbers), and the two are connected.

game2. Kingdom Quiz
What it is: A quiz game that I created between making Gold of a Dragon and The Call of the Void
How it grew: I was given four quiz questions and then I created the fifth quiz question from scratch to learn some code.
What I took from it: The ability to give choice in gaming from just a bit of code is fascinating and can bring out so many possibilities.

game1. Kingdom Battle into The Call of the Void
What it is: A turn-based browser RPG — the foundation that everything else grew from. Heroes face enemies in a fantasy world. You choose actions, the enemy responds, HP bars go down, someone wins.
How it grew: Kingdom Battle started simple and expanded over many sessions. Heroes got backstories and different combat styles. Enemies got vulnerabilities and special moves. A mana system was added so healing and spells cost resources. Equipment started dropping. A skill tree appeared. The world got regions. And eventually it became something I hadn't originally planned — a complete game with a story, an ending, and a reason to play.
My role: I directed every step of the expansion. When something felt wrong — the balance was off, the regions felt identical, the enemies were too easy or too brutal — I said so, and we worked through it. I didn't write most of the code. But I made every design decision about what the game should be and how it should feel.

The world: The Kingdom of Gyona has been consumed by the Void, an ancient darkness that swallowed the capital overnight. Monsters pour out of the corrupted land. Three heroes answered the last distress call. You play as one of them.
What's in it:

Three playable heroes — Aldric the Archmage, Seraphine the Paladin, Kael the Ranger — each with a backstory and different playstyle
A mana system that limits healing and spells, making resource decisions meaningful
25 enemy types across four regions, each with hidden vulnerabilities you discover through play
Equipment that drops after battles — weapons, shields, helmets, chest armour, boots, bracers
A skill tree unlocking one ability every fifth level
A camp system available every fourth battle to rest and review your gear
A Void Lord final boss at the end of the Void region
Procedural sound effects — every action in the game has a unique sound, all generated in code with no audio files needed
A lore intro screen telling the story of Gyona before you pick your hero

What this project represents: This is the thing I built. Not in the sense that I wrote every line — I didn't — but in the sense that I had a vision, I pursued it across many sessions, I caught the problems, I pushed back when things didn't work, and I kept going until it was finished. That's what directing a project means.

Game5. The Call of the Void — Escape (Platformer) — 
What it is: A 2D side-scrolling platformer set in the same world as The Call of the Void. Run, jump, and fight through two corrupted regions of Gyona.
Where I am with this: This is my current frontier. The platformer is working — physics, scrolling camera, enemies, coins, sound effects — but I haven't mastered it yet. I understand the core concepts at a surface level: a continuous game loop, velocity and gravity applied every frame, rectangles checked for overlap. The next step is going deeper and starting to make design decisions the way I did with The Call of the Void.
It's the beginning of something, not the end.

How These Were Built
Every project was built through conversation with Claude — Anthropic's AI assistant.
The honest version of that process: I don't have deep technical knowledge of JavaScript. I have a rudimentary understanding of how the pieces connect — enough to follow what's happening, ask the right questions, and know when something is wrong. The code was written collaboratively. The direction, the design decisions, and the persistence to keep iterating were mine.
What I found is that building something real, even with help, teaches things that reading about code never does. You encounter actual problems. You make decisions that have consequences. You develop a feel for what's possible. That feel is what I'm building.

What's Next
Deeper into the platformer. More levels, more enemy types, eventually a boss fight. And continuing to develop the technical understanding to go alongside the design instincts already built up.
These projects are also the portfolio I'm building while looking for new opportunities — in tech, defense, or anywhere that values someone who learns fast, thinks creatively, and sees things through to the end.

Technical Notes
All games are single HTML files. No frameworks, no build tools, no dependencies. Open in any browser and they run. Sound effects use the Web Audio API — no audio files are loaded, everything is generated in code.
To try any game: download the file and open it in Chrome or Safari.
